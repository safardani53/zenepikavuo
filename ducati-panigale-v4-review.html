Ducati Panigale V4 review: The 20,000 motorbike that's as close as you can get to being MotoGP leg

 Posted on March 24, 2024  |  2 minutes  |  385 words  |  Jenniffer Sheldon

DUCATI claims its new Panigale V4 is “closer to a MotoGP race bike than any other production motorcycle”.

Well, after taking one for a spin it more than lives up to the billing.

But far from being a fire-breathing, collarbone-popping animal, it is easier than ever to ride and ride fast. And boy is it fast.

This Panigale comes in three variants, the V4, V4S and V4 Speciale – which boasts 226hp and a weight of 188kg.

Its predecessor, the 1299 Panigale, is a stunning motorcycle but a flighty, busy thing to wrestle around a racetrack.

In designing the new superbike, Ducati focused on not just increasing performance – but also making that performance accessible to everyone.

To achieve this it had to step away from its beloved V-Twin, and put the V4 engine at the heart of it all.

The engine uses a clever counter-rotating crankshaft to reduce overall gyroscopic forces. This makes the bike turn in with such incredible ease for a 1100cc machine.

Ducati has pushed the bar even higher with the electronic assistance.

The result is a bike with more performance than ever but which does not overwhelm the rider.

There is even a slide control feature, allowing you to choose how far sideways you are happy for the bike to go before the safety net kicks in.

Audi, which owns Ducati, has a reputation for building some blisteringly fast cars that are predictable and easy to drive. And, for the first time, the same is delivered by a Ducati superbike.

It is a feeling of being able to go incredibly fast but without the threat of a hospital visit should you run out of talent.

The hardest bit about the new Ducati Panigale V4 – which starts at £19,250 – is trying to find a reason to fault it.

If we are being super-fussy, the quickshift gear change is a little sluggish if you are not precise with the lever.

Apart from that, Ducati has created if not the impossible, then certainly the improbable. A 214hp bike (or 226 for the Speciale) that doesn’t want to eat you alive. Pretending to be Andrea Dovizioso or Valentino Rossi has never been so easy.
